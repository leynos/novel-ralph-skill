# Save the Cat — genre and beat sheet

Blake Snyder's *Save the Cat* (2005) catalogues ten story types and a
fifteen-beat structure. The framework was developed for screenwriting and ports
cleanly to commercial fiction. Literary fiction can use it too; it just often
inverts or subverts the beats rather than executing them straight.

This reference is the protocol for Phase 6. It produces
`working/plan/genre-stc.md`.

The original framework lives in Snyder's books; this reference gives an
operational summary rather than a substitute. For nuance, consult the source.
Two of the most useful supplements are Jessica Brody's *Save the Cat! Writes a
Novel* (2018), which adapts the screenwriting framework specifically for prose
fiction, and the saves-the-cat.com beat-sheet tools.

## The ten genres

Pick the one that fits the novel. The genre is not "literary" or "thriller" —
those are categories. STC genres are *story types* defined by what the
protagonist faces. A novel can be sold as literary fiction and still be a Rites
of Passage at structural level.

### 1. Monster in the House

A protagonist trapped in a confined space with a destructive force. Horror's
home, but also pandemic novels, siege novels, some thrillers. The confinement
is what generates the structure.

### 2. Golden Fleece

A road or quest, in which the protagonist sets out after something and is
transformed by the journey itself rather than by the object sought. Heist
novels, road novels, many fantasy quests, some immigration narratives.

### 3. Out of the Bottle

The protagonist is granted an extraordinary gift or curse — a power, a wish, a
transformation — and must learn its rules and its cost. Magical realism, some
literary fantasy, many contemporary speculative novels.

### 4. Dude with a Problem

An ordinary protagonist confronts an extraordinary problem they did not seek.
Thrillers, crime fiction, many literary novels about ordinary people meeting
catastrophe.

### 5. Rites of Passage

The protagonist passes through a life-stage transition that forces them to
become a different person. Coming-of-age, midlife, late-life, grief, illness,
divorce, parenthood.

### 6. Buddy Love

Two characters whose connection is the engine of the story — romantic,
platonic, professional, antagonistic. Romance, many literary novels of
friendship, some odd-couple narratives.

### 7. Whydunit

The protagonist investigates a mystery whose answer changes the shape of their
world. Mystery fiction's home, but also many literary novels organised around a
buried truth.

### 8. Fool Triumphant

An apparent fool turns out to know more, or matter more, than the institutional
adults around them. Picaresque, comic novels, some satirical fiction.

### 9. Institutionalized

The protagonist's struggle is with a system — workplace, family, military,
religion, government, profession. Institutional novels, family sagas, many
political and satirical novels.

### 10. Superhero

The protagonist is extraordinary in some way that sets them apart, and the
novel is about the burden and meaning of that extraordinariness. Not just
superhero fiction — also many novels of genius, prodigy, athletic talent, and
so on.

### How to select

Match the protagonist's structural situation, not the surface register. A
literary novel about a woman returning to her hometown after her mother's death
may sit in Rites of Passage, Whydunit, or Institutionalized depending on what
actually drives the plot. Be honest about which engine is doing the work.

Document the choice with a paragraph of justification grounded in the premise
and treatment.

## The fifteen-beat sheet

The beats run in order. They are anchored to percentages of the total work.
Adapted from Snyder's screenplay percentages to novel structure.

The percentages are guides, not rules. A novel may compress or expand any beat;
what matters is that all fifteen happen and in order.

| #   | Beat                   | Position | Function                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| --- | ---------------------- | -------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Opening Image          | 0–1%     | The first impression. Tone, voice, world. Should mirror or contrast the Final Image.                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| 2   | Theme Stated           | 4–5%     | A line or scene that articulates what the novel is about, often in passing. The protagonist usually doesn't notice.                                                                                                                                                               |
| 3   | Set-Up                 | 1–10%    | The protagonist's status quo. Their attractor, their world, their flaws, the relationships in place.                                                                                                                                                                              |
| 4   | Catalyst               | 10%      | The event that disturbs the status quo. The phone call, the death, the offer, the meeting.                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| 5   | Debate                 | 10–20%   | The protagonist's resistance to acting on the catalyst. Should I? Can I? What if?                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| 6   | Break into Two         | 20–25%   | The protagonist commits. They cross a threshold. Act II begins.                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| 7   | B Story                | 25–30%   | A second narrative thread, often a relationship, that will carry the thematic weight. The B story is the heart of the novel.                                                                                                                                                      |
| 8   | Fun and Games          | 25–50%   | The "promise of the premise". The cool stuff the reader came for. If the book is sold on a heist, this is the heist's planning and early execution.                                                                                                                               |
| 9   | Midpoint               | 50%      | A major reversal. A false victory or false defeat that changes the protagonist's understanding of the situation. The stakes raise.                                                                                                                                                |
| 10  | Bad Guys Close In      | 50–75%   | The forces opposing the protagonist tighten. Internal fractures emerge. Allies wobble.                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| 11  | All Is Lost            | 75%      | The lowest point. The plan has failed. A death (literal or symbolic) marks the moment.                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| 12  | Dark Night of the Soul | 75–80%   | The protagonist sits with the loss. What does it mean? What did they fail to see? The thematic crisis.                                                                                                                                                                            |
| 13  | Break into Three       | 80%      | The protagonist finds the new approach — usually requiring the integration of the B story's lesson — and commits. Act III begins.                                                                                                                                                 |
| 14  | Finale                 | 80–99%   | The climax sequence. Protagonist executes the new approach, confronts the antagonistic force, resolves the central conflict. Often broken into five sub-beats: gathering the team, executing the plan, the high tower surprise, the dig deep down, the execution of the new plan. |
| 15  | Final Image            | 99–100%  | The last impression. Mirrors or contrasts the Opening Image. Shows what has changed.                                                                                                                                                                                              |

## Populating the beat sheet for this novel

For each of the fifteen beats:

1. **Name the beat** in the form: *Beat name — short label for what
   happens.* Example: *Catalyst — the letter arrives*.
2. **Target word count** as both an absolute number and a
   percentage. Sum across beats should equal the target word count ± 10%.
3. **Description.** One to three paragraphs. Names which
   characters appear, what happens, what changes by the beat's end. Specific
   enough that a chapter could be planned against it.

## Beat-to-treatment crosswalk

After populating the beats, verify alignment with the treatment:

| Treatment event        | Beat #       |
| ---------------------- | ------------ |
| [event from treatment] | [which beat] |
| …                      | …            |

Two failure modes to surface:

- **Orphan event in treatment.** An event the treatment names
  but no beat covers. Either the event is filler (cut from treatment) or a beat
  needs to expand to include it.
- **Orphan beat.** A beat the treatment doesn't reach. Either
  the treatment is incomplete or the beat is forced — the framework wants it
  but the story doesn't.

Resolve all orphans before exiting Phase 6.

`working/plan/genre-stc.md`:

```markdown

## Genre

**Selected genre:** <one of the ten>

**Justification:** <one paragraph grounded in premise and

## Beat sheet

**Position:** 0–1% (target: <N> words)
**Label:** <short>
**Description:** <prose>
**Characters present:** <names>
**State change:** <if any>

### Beat 2 — Theme Stated
...

[All fifteen beats.]

## Crosswalk

| Treatment event | Beat # |
|---|---|
| ... | ... |

## Notes

[Any beat where the structure is being deliberately bent. Any
beat that's risky and may need extra attention during drafting.]
```

## On using STC for literary fiction

Literary fiction often resists the framework on aesthetic grounds. The agent
should still complete it. Several useful outputs:

- **Beats can be inverted.** The All Is Lost moment in some
  literary novels is a false victory; the Finale is a retreat. The framework
  still applies — the protagonist still passes through these structural
  positions, just with inverted valence.
- **The B story may be the A story.** Many literary novels are
  structured around a relationship while a surface plot pretends to be the
  engine. Identify the actual engine in Phase 6 and name it correctly.
- **The Final Image may refute the Opening Image rather than
  resolving it.** This is not a beat sheet failure; it's a legitimate literary
  move. Document it.

The framework is a diagnostic, not a corset. Use it to understand the novel's
structure, not to bend the novel into a template.

## Anti-patterns

- **Genre cargo-culting.** Picking a genre because the surface
  matches without checking that the structural engine matches.
- **Skipping beats that feel "commercial".** All fifteen beats
  happen in any working novel. They may be subdued, inverted, or brief, but
  they happen. If a beat is genuinely absent, the novel has a structural
  problem.
- **Forcing the beat to a percentage.** The percentages are
  guides. A long Set-Up is fine in a slow literary novel. A short Set-Up is
  fine in a propulsive thriller. The order matters more than the spacing.
- **Cutting the B story.** Whatever you call it, the second
  thematic thread carries the meaning of the novel. Cutting it produces a flat
  trajectory.
