# Desloppify checklist

AI prose has a tell set as recognisable as a perfume. The model has been
trained on the internet and reaches by default for the prose attractors most
rewarded by RLHF: balanced, sonorous, em-dashed, abstract, tricolon-heavy,
motion-blur emotional. The pattern is audible at a hundred paces.

This checklist exists to find and kill the tells before the spiteful critic
does. It runs after the beats are written and before the critic sees the
chapter. It is not optional. It is not cosmetic.

## How to run

For each item below: scan the chapter for the pattern. For each hit, decide
between (a) cut, (b) rewrite into the writer's actual register, or (c) keep
with explicit justification (rare). Document in `log.md` only if a hit is kept
against the rule.

The checklist is divided into:

1. Sentence-level tells
2. Paragraph-level tells
3. Chapter-level tells
4. Dialogue tells
5. Register tells
6. The high-frequency offenders

## 1. Sentence-level tells

### Em-dash flooding

The em dash is one of the most overused punctuation marks in LLM-generated
prose. Three em dashes per page is suspicious; five is incriminating. Replace
most with commas, semicolons, or full stops. Reserve the em dash for genuine
interruption or parenthetical aside that earns the typographic weight.

Note: this skill's parent voice (df12) uses **spaced en dashes** ( – ) in
prose, not unspaced em dashes (—). If writing in the df12 register, enforce
that as well.

### "It's not just X, it's Y."

The most recognisable LLM rhetorical move. A pretend-argument that sounds like
clarification but adds nothing. Variants: "Not merely X but Y." "Not just X —
Y." Every instance gets cut or rewritten.

### Tricolons of abstraction

Three abstract nouns in parallel, ascending: "the silence, the weight, the
unbearable knowing." This is incantatory padding. Pick one. Make it concrete.

### Sentence-initial gerund stacking

"Standing there, holding the letter, watching the rain, she…" The participial
pile-up is a default LLM cadence. Untangle into sequence: "She stood there. She
held the letter. She watched the rain."

### "Found herself" passives

"She found herself crying." "He found himself agreeing." A passive construction
smuggled in to avoid committing the character to the action. Make it active:
"She cried." "He agreed."

### "Couldn't help but"

"She couldn't help but smile." Same as above. Either she smiled or she didn't.
The hedge protects the writer from the reader's judgement of an unearned smile.

### "Let out a breath she didn't know she was holding"

A specific cliché so worn it has its own meme. Cut. Cut variants too: "released
a breath", "exhaled the breath she'd been holding."

### "Shivers down [her] spine"

Cliché. Cut. Same for "her blood ran cold", "her heart skipped a beat", "a
chill ran through her", "goosebumps prickled her skin."

### "The air was thick with"

Cliché. Cut. Same for "the silence stretched", "the tension was palpable", "the
room held its breath."

### Capitalised abstract nouns

"There was a Knowing in her eyes." "The Truth, when it came, was Heavy." The
capitalisation is a sentimentality flag. Decapitalise and consider whether the
abstraction earns its place at all.

### Sentence-initial "Listen", "Here's the thing", "I want you to"

LLM tonal markers. Almost never appropriate in prose fiction. Cut.

### Stacked adverbs

"She walked slowly, carefully, deliberately toward the door." One adverb is
usually too many. Three is parody. Choose the most specific, or replace with a
verb that carries the connotation ("crept", "stalked", "edged").

### Empty intensifiers

"absolutely", "truly", "simply", "really", "actually" used to boost a claim the
prose has not earned. Cut.

### "—but that was just the beginning."

Chapter-end cliffhanger gesture. Almost always lazy. Cut. End on the actual
last action; let the cliff edge form itself.

### Mirror-gaze self-description

"She caught her reflection in the window and saw a woman she barely
recognised." A way to describe the protagonist without giving her a witness.
Almost always replaceable with something more interesting.

### "A part of her…"

"A part of her wanted to scream. A part of her wanted to stay." Internal
fragmentation as a substitute for a decisive interior. Either commit to the
conflicted interior with specificity or pick one and let the other emerge
through behaviour.

## 2. Paragraph-level tells

### The motion-blur emotional paragraph

Three or four sentences of abstract emotional reportage:

> Everything in her felt heavy. The world seemed very far away. She
> didn't know how she was supposed to do this. The weight of it all
> pressed down on her until she could barely breathe.

This is a category of LLM filler that does no narrative work. Cut entirely or
replace with one concrete action or sensation.

### The setting-as-mood paragraph

A paragraph of weather and architecture that exists to set tone rather than to
be observed by a character or to drive the scene. Cut unless the description
does specific work; replace with one sharp detail rendered through the POV
character.

### The recap paragraph

A paragraph that summarises earlier events for the reader's benefit. The reader
doesn't need it; they read those events. Cut. If a reminder is necessary, find
one image or line that resurfaces it.

### The thematic announcement

A paragraph in which the prose tells the reader what the chapter means. "This
was what it meant to lose someone, to lose so much that you weren't sure who
was left." The reader extracts theme from action; the prose does not need to
extract it for them. Cut.

### The triple-image paragraph

Three sensory details delivered in parallel construction to evoke atmosphere.
"The smell of woodsmoke. The taste of metal on the air. The sound, far off, of
a dog refusing to settle." This is fine occasionally; it becomes a tic if it
appears more than once or twice per chapter. Check frequency.

## 3. Chapter-level tells

### The chapter that does not change state

If the protagonist ends the chapter where they began, in possession of the same
information, with the same relationship network and the same emotional
position, the chapter is filler. Cut it or rewrite to land somewhere new.

### The chapter with two endings

A chapter that climaxes, settles into resolution, then climaxes again. Pick one
ending. Move the other to its own chapter.

### The opening that doesn't open

Chapters that start with weather, with the protagonist waking up, with a long
internal monologue, with a flashback, or with exposition framed as memory. Open
on action or on a line of dialogue that does work. Save the weather for
paragraph two.

### Closing-line sententiousness

"And in that moment, she understood." "Nothing would ever be the same again."
"Some things, once broken, cannot be put back together." These are the LLM's
default chapter-end gestures. Find a closing image or line that does the same
work without telling the reader how to feel.

### The exposition route via dialogue

Two characters who already know X explain X to each other for the reader's
benefit. "As you know, the council meets every year." Re-route the exposition:
render it through observation, through inference, through a third party who
plausibly doesn't know, or cut it.

## 4. Dialogue tells

### "X said" with adverbs

"'I don't think so,' she said sadly." Almost always replaceable with "she said"
plus action ("she said. Her hand went to the window frame.") or no tag at all
where the speaker is clear.

### Speech-act inflation

"'Get out,' he snarled." "'Hello,' she purred." Real people say. Occasionally
they whisper or shout. They rarely snarl, purr, hiss, growl, or breathe their
lines. Replace 80% of these with "said".

### All characters speak in the same voice

If you can't tell who is talking without a tag, the voices are not
differentiated. Re-read the character voice notes and rewrite.

### Dialogue without subtext

Every line says exactly what it means. Real conversations contain deflection,
evasion, performance, miscommunication. If a scene reads as direct exchange of
information, layer in subtext.

### Dialogue that explains the writer's research

"As you know, in 1923, the treaty…" Smuggling research into character mouths.
Cut or relocate.

## 5. Register tells

### Anachronism

A modern phrase in a historical setting, or a historical phrase in a modern
setting. Search for words that ring against the era of the book.

### Voice slippage

The narrator's voice drifting between chapters. Free indirect discourse should
sound like the POV character's idiolect, not the writer's. If chapter 3's
narrator sounds like chapter 7's different POV narrator, the voice is the
writer's, not the character's.

### Tonal inconsistency within a scene

A scene that opens in observational realism and closes in metaphysical reverie
without earning the transition. Either earn the shift or pick a register and
hold it.

### The wikipedia voice

A passage where the prose suddenly adopts a third-person encyclopaedic register
to convey factual content. "The town of X had been founded in Y, when…" If
world-building is required, route it through the POV character's perception,
knowledge, or ignorance.

## 6. The high-frequency offenders

Quick-scan list. For each, count occurrences in the chapter. More than three is
a tic; more than five is a problem.

| Phrase                             | Threshold  |
| ---------------------------------- | ---------- |
| "It's not just… it's…"             | 0          |
| "let out a breath"                 | 0          |
| "shivers down [her] spine"         | 0          |
| "the air was thick with"           | 0          |
| "found [herself]" + verb           | 2          |
| "couldn't help but"                | 1          |
| em dash                            | 5 per page |
| "—but that was just the beginning" | 0          |
| "And in that moment"               | 0          |
| "Some things…" (sententious)       | 0          |
| "a part of her"                    | 1          |
| capitalised abstract noun          | 1          |
| "smirked"                          | 1          |
| "[verb]-ed sadly/quietly/softly"   | 2          |
| "the silence stretched"            | 0          |
| "her heart skipped"                | 0          |
| "every fibre of her being"         | 0          |
| "in a world where"                 | 0          |
| "tapestry of"                      | 0          |
| "symphony of"                      | 0          |
| "a delicate balance"               | 0          |
| "navigate the complexities"        | 0          |
| "speaks volumes"                   | 0          |
| "paradigm shift"                   | 0          |

The thresholds are deliberately tight. Most are zero because the phrase is
dead. The few non-zero thresholds reflect that the phrase has legitimate uses,
but only at the margin.

This §6 table is shipped as the `offenders.toml` rule pack, which `desloppify`
loads by default. The broader lexical *AI-isms* — phrasal tells such as
"load-bearing" and "a testament to" — are now enforceable too, via a second
pack, `novel_ralph_skill/rulepack/packs/ai-isms.toml`. It is opt-in: run
`desloppify --pack novel_ralph_skill/rulepack/packs/ai-isms.toml` to scan a
chapter against it. The pack is versioned data the maintainer keeps current; its
update cadence and membership policy live in the developers' guide ("Rule packs
and the loader boundary"). Keep working this prose checklist as well; the pack
mechanizes only the lexical subset, not the paragraph- and chapter-level tells.

## Tone of the desloppify pass

The pass is destructive. It removes more than it adds. If the chapter loses
10–20% of its word count to desloppification, that is normal. If it loses 0–2%,
the pass is being too gentle; re-run with more aggression.

The pass does not aim for perfection. It aims to remove the layer of
LLM-default ornament that obscures the actual chapter underneath. The spiteful
critic will catch the rest.
