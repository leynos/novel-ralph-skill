# JTBD for fiction

Product-market fit, jobs-to-be-done, and opportunity/solution
space exploration are normally the vocabulary of software
businesses. Applied to a novel, they look like a category error,
but they are not. They are a discipline that forces honesty
about who the book is for and what work it does in their life.

The discipline catches the most common failure of long-form
fiction: the indulgent passion project that no identifiable
reader wants to read. Catching this at Phase 5 saves the agent
from drafting eighty thousand words of work for an empty
audience.

This reference is the protocol for Phase 5. It produces
`working/reader/audience.md` and `working/reader/comps.md`.

## Concepts

### Reader

The specific person the novel is for. Not "readers of literary
fiction"; not "fans of speculative fiction"; a person you could
recognise. Their reading habits, what shelf they pull from, how
they discover books, how much time they have, what they read
last and last loved.

The reader does not have to exist in the singular. The novel may
serve several reader archetypes. But each one must be drawn
specifically enough to be falsifiable. A reader who could be
anyone is no one.

### Job-to-be-done

What the reader hires the novel to do in their life. The JTBD
framework distinguishes:

- **Functional job.** The practical work the novel performs.
  Pass the time on a flight; learn something; sharpen a skill
  (writers reading widely); access a world the reader cannot
  enter (historical fiction, immersive fantasy); supply a
  reading project.
- **Emotional job.** The feeling state the novel offers.
  Catharsis, escape, dread, comfort, recognition, anger, calm,
  thrill, melancholy, wit.
- **Social job.** The role the novel plays in the reader's
  social life. Discussable at book club; impressive on a
  shelf; recommendable to a specific friend; shareable on
  social media; a quiet pleasure not for sharing.

A serious novel does at least one functional job, at least one
emotional job, and usually one social job. State all three.

### Opportunity space

The unmet or underserved demand in the reader's life that the
novel addresses. "Where the reader is shopping and not finding
what they want." Opportunities exist where:

- A genre's recent offerings have grown formulaic.
- A demographic of readers is over-served by one register and
  under-served by another (e.g., women over 40 reading thrillers
  written assuming a younger protagonist).
- A reader-life-condition has no canonical novel (single
  parenthood, late-career disillusion, post-illness
  recalibration).
- A tonal register is missing (literary horror that is funny;
  political fiction that is not solemn).
- A topical concern lacks a novelistic treatment (a recent
  technological shift, social rupture, generational handover).

Naming the opportunity space sharpens the novel. If no
opportunity can be named, the novel is competing in an
oversupplied market and will need exceptional execution to
matter.

### Comps

Comparable published works the novel sits alongside. Comps are
not aspirational and not denigrating. They are positioning:
"this novel is in the conversation with these others." Three to
five comps is standard. Each comp:

- Should be published, not unpublished.
- Should be within the last ten years for most categories
  (longer for established literary fiction; shorter for
  trend-driven categories).
- Should share at least two of: reader, tone, subject, scale,
  formal approach, register, length.
- Should not be widely successful in every comp slot (a debut
  novel comping itself to *Hamnet* and *Demon Copperhead* and
  *Sea of Tranquillity* is overreaching; one of these is fine
  for tone, the others should be smaller).

### Anti-comps

Books the novel is *not* trying to be, named to clarify
positioning by contrast. Anti-comps can be widely successful
books in adjacent space whose approach this novel rejects. They
help the agent and the writer avoid drift toward the wrong
attractor during drafting.

## Protocol

### Step 1 — Sketch the reader

Write a portrait. Roughly three hundred words. The agent answers,
without abstracting:

- Age range, life stage, region (if relevant).
- What they read in the last year. Three titles minimum.
- Where they read (commute, bedtime, holidays, between
  meetings).
- How they discover books (recommendations, reviews, bookshop
  browsing, social media, algorithm).
- What they don't read and why.
- What's frustrating about their current reading life.

If the agent cannot produce this portrait, the novel does not
yet know who it is for. Pause. Either narrow the premise toward
a reader or proceed with the question marked unresolved and
revisit at the 30% knitting circle.

### Step 2 — Name the JTBD

In one sentence each, state:

- The functional job.
- The emotional job.
- The social job.

If any job is "to entertain", be more specific. Entertainment is
the medium; the job is what kind of entertainment.

Wrong: "The reader hires this novel for entertainment."
Right: "The reader hires this novel for ninety minutes of
absorbed evening reading that delivers dread without horror's
explicit register."

### Step 3 — Name the opportunity space

In one to two paragraphs, identify the gap. What is the reader
not getting from what is currently on the shelf? Why is this
novel positioned to fill that gap?

If the answer is "this novel is just very good", that is not an
opportunity. Execution quality is not a market position. Try
again.

### Step 4 — Name the comps

Three to five published works, each with:

- Author, title, year, publisher.
- Brief note on which axes this novel shares ground with the
  comp.

Verify the comps are real. (Use web search if uncertain about
publishers or years. Imaginary comps are an own goal.)

### Step 5 — Name the anti-comps

Two or three. Same format as comps, plus a sentence on what this
novel is doing differently and why.

### Step 6 — Self-test

Apply these tests:

- **The bookshop test.** A bookseller asks "what's it like?" The
  agent answers in one sentence using two comps and a tonal
  word. If the answer comes out as a marketing logline, retry.
  The answer should sound like one human telling another about
  a book.
- **The recommendation test.** Name one specific person the
  reader of this novel would press it into the hands of. If no
  one would press it on anyone, the novel may have no social
  job — which is acceptable but should be acknowledged.
- **The shelf test.** Stand the novel between its comps on a
  shelf. Does it look like it belongs? If yes, position is
  good. If no, either the comps are wrong or the novel is
  drifting from its market.

## Output format

`working/reader/audience.md`:

```markdown
# Reader and JTBD

## Reader profile
<three-hundred-word portrait>

## Functional job
<one sentence>

## Emotional job
<one sentence>

## Social job
<one sentence>

## Opportunity space
<one to two paragraphs naming the unmet demand>

## Bookshop pitch
<one sentence the way one human tells another about a book>
```

`working/reader/comps.md`:

```markdown
# Comparable works

## Comps

### <Author>, *<Title>* (<Year>, <Publisher>)
**Shares:** <axes>
**Note:** <one sentence>

[Three to five entries.]

## Anti-comps

### <Author>, *<Title>* (<Year>, <Publisher>)
**Avoids:** <what this novel does differently>
**Why:** <one sentence>

[Two or three entries.]
```

## When the analysis falsifies the premise

Phase 5 is the second phase capable of falsifying earlier work
(after Phase 3). Common breakages:

- **No reader can be drawn.** The novel is for the writer alone.
  Two options: revise the premise toward a reader, or proceed
  with eyes open. Both are valid. Pretending the question wasn't
  asked is not.
- **The JTBD is filled by a hundred other books.** Either find a
  unique angle the novel takes on the same job, or revise the
  premise.
- **No opportunity space.** The market is saturated. Same
  options as above.
- **No comps fit.** Either the novel is genuinely innovative (in
  which case find one or two adjacent comps anyway and note the
  divergence) or the novel is not yet legible enough to
  position. The second case is more common.

These corrections happen now. The cost of a falsified premise at
Phase 5 is a few hours; the cost of a falsified premise at
chapter 18 is the novel.

## A note on commercial vs. literary positioning

JTBD analysis is sometimes resisted by writers of literary
fiction as commercial reductionism. It is not. Literary fiction
has readers, jobs, opportunities, and comps as surely as
commercial fiction does. *The Bee Sting* has a reader; *A Little
Life* has a reader; *Beautyland* has a reader. Their JTBDs are
unusual ones — moral seriousness, emotional intensity, formal
ambition — but they are JTBDs.

If the writer's instinct is to refuse this framing, the agent
should still complete it. The output may be one of:

- "This novel is for readers who hire it to do what *Author X*
  used to do but no longer does."
- "This novel is for the reader who is tired of how the
  contemporary literary novel handles X."
- "This novel is for the reader who wants the difficulty of X
  without the bleakness of Y."

A literary novel that cannot answer the question at all is in
trouble.
